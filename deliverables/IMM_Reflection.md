# Interactive Memory Machine — Reflection

**Team 10 · SUTD · Term 6 · Creative Machine Learning**
Jiawen · Naing Thway · Vrinda · Zaw Ye Htet (primary developer)
2026-04-29

---

In our final project, we critically evaluated the artifacts produced by the Interactive Memory Machine (IMM) by comparing them against three reference points: the original recall texts written by each subject, the wiring patterns each subject authored on the node canvas, and our intended architectural categories — sculptural mass, structural members, and printable form. As a team, we found that the analysis stage, in which Claude extracts keywords, scores twelve distortion parameters, and classifies a tone archetype, was generally effective at lifting the qualitative content of the recall into a structured, machine-readable specification. Repeated rhythms, spatial cues, and emotional tone were consistently surfaced. However, when the resulting mechanical specification was passed downstream to the text-to-image model, the result did not always preserve the specific character of the recall. The numbers were captured; the *recall* was sometimes not.

Many of the AI-generated 2D artifacts initially appeared generic, often resembling familiar generative-art forms — clean parametric sculpture, glossy product-render aesthetics — rather than designs that were clearly authored by the subject's wiring. This pointed us to a gap between *parameter extraction* and *visual translation*. The image model could see that distortion was specified, but it did not always understand how a 0.81 stability score should manifest as a tilted suspended mass, or how a misassociation wiring should produce two incompatible bodies fused at a single seam. To close this gap we introduced the *art director* — a second Claude pass that reads the original recall, the archetype, all twelve scores, and the wiring pattern, and rewrites the mechanical specification into a single cohesive visual brief. The art director is the most consequential decision in our pipeline. Without it, the image model treated our specs as noise; with it, the specs became architectural intent.

The 3D reconstruction stage exposed a different kind of distortion. fal.ai Trellis and Hunyuan3D V3 lift a 2D image into a continuous mesh, but they also impose their own priors — meshes tend to close holes, smooth jagged edges, and round off shards. Patterns that had strong glitch logic at the 2D stage could be reduced to coherent, well-mannered sculptural blobs by the time they reached the GLB. Through evaluation, we recognised that AI outputs at every stage of our pipeline cannot be treated as final design results. They require careful filtering, discussion, and re-wiring. We assessed each artifact on three criteria: how closely it retained the recall's spatial logic, whether the wiring pattern was legible in the form (could a viewer reverse-engineer the operation pulls from the artifact alone?), and whether the mesh remained physically plausible — a sculpture rather than an abstract 2D-only composition. Although these tools were helpful in producing variations rapidly, the project showed us that human judgement remains essential in identifying when a generation has drifted away from the subject's intent and in steering the wiring back toward more meaningful architectural outcomes.

Overall, our project demonstrated that ML can serve as a credible *translator layer* between subjective spatial recall and generative form — but only when the pipeline preserves authorship at the points where it matters. The node canvas is the load-bearing decision in IMM: it is what makes the distortion the *subject's* distortion rather than the model's. Three of our open questions, which we did not have time to test before the crit, are: whether different subjects recalling the same physical space produce convergent or divergent artifacts; whether the same subject's artifact for the same space drifts as their memory mutates over time; and whether this pipeline can support real architectural briefs for spaces meant to be forgotten, reassembled, or grieved. The IMM is, in this sense, a sketch of a method rather than a finished tool — but the sketch is sharp enough to take into the next iteration.
